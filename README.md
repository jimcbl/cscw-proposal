# What Counts as Success in Civic Technology?

## A CSCW Study of Evaluation Metrics, Cooperative Work, and Sustainability

## 1. Introduction

Civic technology projects are often developed to support public participation, community action, institutional transparency, or collaboration between citizens and public organizations. Examples include citizen reporting platforms, participatory budgeting tools, community mapping systems, and open-source platforms for public-service innovation. However, many of these projects define success through visible and easily countable indicators, such as number of users, number of reports submitted, number of platform visits, number of issues solved, or whether a prototype was deployed.

While these metrics are useful, they may overlook an important CSCW concern: whether the technology actually supports cooperative work. A civic technology platform may receive many reports from citizens, but still fail if public officials do not respond, if community moderators perform invisible unpaid labor, or if participation stops after the initial pilot. Therefore, the practical problem is that civic tech success is often measured through platform activity, while the cooperative, organizational, and maintenance work behind civic participation remains underexamined.

This problem is relevant to CSCW because CSCW studies the relationship between technical systems and cooperative practices. Ackerman's “intellectual challenge of CSCW” highlights the gap between social requirements and technical feasibility, while CSCW work on invisible work, articulation work, participation, awareness, and coordination provides useful concepts for examining civic technology beyond technical adoption metrics.

### Research Question

**How do civic technology projects define and evaluate success, and to what extent do these success metrics capture cooperative work, citizen participation, invisible labor, and long-term sustainability?**

### Potential Contribution to CSCW

This study contributes to CSCW by showing how success metrics in civic technology make some forms of cooperative work visible while hiding others. It may help develop a more CSCW-sensitive understanding of civic tech evaluation, where success is not only measured by usage or deployment, but also by coordination, participation quality, institutional response, maintenance work, and sustainability.

## 2. Background and Theoretical Framing

This proposal is grounded in CSCW theories of cooperative work, invisible labor, participation, coordination, and socio-technical design.

First, Ackerman's work on the gap between social requirements and technical feasibility is useful for understanding why civic tech platforms may appear successful technically while failing socially or organizationally. A platform may collect citizen input efficiently, but the broader social requirement is not only data collection; it is meaningful cooperation between citizens, developers, community organizers, and institutions.

Second, Star and Strauss's concept of visible and invisible work helps frame the hidden labor behind civic technology. Civic tech systems often depend on activities such as moderation, onboarding, translation, explaining the platform to citizens, maintaining data quality, following up with institutions, and resolving conflicts. These forms of work may be essential to the success of a project but absent from official success metrics.

Third, Strauss's articulation work is relevant because civic tech requires coordination across different actors and responsibilities. Citizens report issues, developers maintain the platform, organizers mobilize participation, and institutions may or may not act on the information. Evaluation metrics that only measure user activity may ignore this coordination work.

Fourth, CSCW research on participation and participatory design helps question what “citizen participation” actually means. Bratteteig and Wagner's work on participation shows that participation is not a simple binary between included and excluded users. In civic tech, citizens may be treated only as data providers, or they may have real influence over design, decision-making, and governance.

Fifth, coordination and awareness are central CSCW concerns. Work by Schmidt, Gross, Cabitza, Simone, and others shows that cooperative systems must support coordination, flexibility, and awareness in real work settings. This is important for civic tech because successful civic participation often depends on whether actors understand who is responsible, what actions have been taken, and what remains unresolved.

Finally, sustainability is important because many civic tech projects remain prototypes or pilots. A CSCW perspective suggests that sustainability is not only a technical issue, but also a matter of long-term cooperative arrangements, maintenance, institutional commitment, and community capacity.

This study therefore treats success metrics not as neutral measurements, but as socio-technical artifacts that shape what kinds of civic work are valued, counted, and ignored.

## 3. Planned Methods

### Research Design

We propose a small qualitative case study of civic technology projects. The study will examine how success is defined, measured, and experienced by different actors involved in civic tech.

The study will focus on **two or three civic technology projects**, preferably projects that are publicly documented and still accessible online. Possible cases include:

1. a citizen reporting platform,
2. a participatory budgeting platform,
3. an open-source civic tech project on GitHub, or
4. a community mapping or civic data platform.

A case study design is appropriate because the research question focuses on how evaluation practices are embedded in real cooperative settings, rather than testing a predefined hypothesis.

### Data Generation Methods

This is an empirical study, not a literature review. We will generate data using three methods.

#### 1. Document and Platform Analysis

We will collect public project materials, such as:

- project websites,
- annual reports,
- impact reports,
- GitHub README files,
- GitHub issues and pull requests,
- public documentation,
- blog posts,
- public dashboards or statistics.

We will examine how each project describes success. For example, we will identify whether the project emphasizes user numbers, technical deployment, number of reports, community engagement, policy impact, institutional response, or long-term maintenance.

#### 2. Semi-Structured Interviews

We will conduct approximately **4–6 short interviews** with people involved in civic tech projects. These may include:

- developers,
- project organizers,
- community moderators,
- civic activists,
- public-sector partners,
- active users.

The interviews will explore questions such as:

- How do you know whether the project is successful?
- What metrics or indicators do you use?
- What work is necessary to keep the project running?
- What forms of work are not captured by official metrics?
- Who is responsible for responding to citizen input?
- What happens after citizens participate?
- What makes the project sustainable or difficult to sustain?

#### 3. Small Comparative Metric Mapping

For each case, we will create a simple table comparing:

| Category | Example Question |
|---|---|
| Official success metrics | What does the project publicly measure? |
| Cooperative work | What collaboration is needed for the project to function? |
| Invisible work | What work is essential but not counted? |
| Participation quality | Are citizens data providers, testers, co-designers, or decision-makers? |
| Institutional response | Does citizen input lead to action? |
| Sustainability | Is the project maintained after launch? |

This mapping will help us compare how success is defined across cases.

### Data Analysis

We will use qualitative thematic analysis. First, each researcher will independently code a small subset of data. Then the group will discuss differences and refine a shared coding scheme. Possible initial codes include:

- usage-based success,
- technical success,
- participation success,
- institutional response,
- invisible work,
- moderation work,
- maintenance work,
- coordination breakdown,
- sustainability,
- citizen influence,
- project abandonment.

To improve rigor, we will keep an audit trail of coding decisions, write short analytic memos, and compare interpretations among group members. This follows CSCW and HCI concerns about transparency and reliability in qualitative research, including the need to explain how coding decisions are made rather than presenting themes as purely objective findings.

## 4. Reflection

### Research Paradigm

This study follows an interpretivist and socio-technical research paradigm. We do not assume that success metrics are objective or neutral. Instead, we treat them as social and organizational choices that reflect what project actors value. Our aim is to understand how different actors define success and how these definitions shape cooperative civic work.

The study is also aligned with CSCW's practice-oriented tradition. Rather than studying technology alone, we examine how technology is embedded in everyday work practices, responsibilities, and institutional arrangements.

### Ethical Considerations

The study involves low-risk qualitative research, but there are still important ethical issues.

First, interview participants must give informed consent. They should understand the purpose of the study, what data will be collected, how the data will be used, and that they can withdraw at any time.

Second, some participants may discuss problems in their organizations, such as failed projects, poor institutional response, unpaid labor, or conflict between volunteers and public institutions. Therefore, we should anonymize participants and avoid naming individuals unless they explicitly agree.

Third, if we analyze public GitHub issues or online discussions, we should still treat contributors ethically. Even if data is public, users may not expect their posts to be analyzed in a research project. We should avoid quoting sensitive or identifiable comments directly unless necessary.

Fourth, we should be reflexive about our own position. As researchers interested in civic tech, we may assume that participation is always positive. However, CSCW reminds us that participation can create extra work, conflict, exclusion, or unequal responsibility. Guillemin and Gillam's idea of “ethically important moments” is relevant because ethical issues may emerge during fieldwork, not only during formal approval stages.

### Necessary Approvals

Depending on the university's rules, we may need ethics approval before conducting interviews. If full ethics approval is not required for a class project, we will still prepare:

- an informed consent form,
- a short project information sheet,
- an interview protocol,
- a data management plan.

We will store interview notes securely, remove personal identifiers, and only share anonymized excerpts in the final report.

### How the Work Should Be Evaluated

This work should not be evaluated by whether it produces statistically generalizable findings. Instead, it should be evaluated by:

- clarity of the research question,
- quality of the empirical data,
- transparency of the coding process,
- connection to CSCW theory,
- ethical handling of participants and public data,
- usefulness of the findings for understanding civic tech evaluation.

A successful outcome would be a grounded CSCW analysis showing how current success metrics capture some aspects of civic technology while overlooking cooperative work, invisible labor, and sustainability.
